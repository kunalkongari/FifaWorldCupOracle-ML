Pipeline overview

  • Web scraping — 928 World Cup match results (1930–2022) scraped from Wikipedia using BeautifulSoup + lxml. The 2026 fixture was pinned to a Wayback Machine snapshot for reproducibility.
  • ELO ratings — computed iteratively over all 928 matches. Every team starts at 1500. Wins earn points, losses lose them. Larger wins (by goal difference) produce bigger updates. A yearly decay factor (×0.85 of gap from 1500) ensures recent form outweighs distant history.
  • Feature engineering — 9 features built strictly from pre-match data to prevent leakage: ELO ratings, ELO difference, 15-match rolling form (goals scored & conceded), and head-to-head win/draw rates.
  • XGBoost classifier — 300 trees, max depth 3, learning rate 0.04, with class-weight balancing to handle the draw class imbalance. Predicts Home Win / Draw / Away Win probabilities.
  • Hybrid blend — final probabilities = 70% XGBoost + 30% Poisson. The Poisson component uses rolling attacking/defensive form to anchor extreme predictions.
  • Monte Carlo simulation — 10,000 full tournament simulations. Each match outcome is sampled from the hybrid model's predicted distribution. A probability cache pre-computes all pairings for speed.

Model performance

  • 5-fold stratified CV accuracy: 51.5%
  • Random baseline (3 outcomes): 33.3%
  • Lift over random: +18.2 pp
  • Training data: 928 World Cup matches, 1930–2022
  • Historical outcome split: ~54.6% Home wins · ~22.2% Draws · ~23.2% Away wins
  • Class imbalance handled via compute_sample_weight('balanced')

Key design decisions

  • Why XGBoost over deep learning? — With only 928 training samples, gradient-boosted trees with regularisation outperform neural networks. XGBoost is the standard choice for tabular data at this scale.
  • Why ELO over average goals? — Average goals ignore opponent quality. ELO updates after every match and accounts for who you beat, not just by how much.
  • Why 10,000 simulations? — Converges within ±0.5 pp of the true model probability for each team's win chance, which is the professional standard for tournament simulation.
  • Why World Cup data only? — Qualification matches and friendlies carry different tactical contexts. Using only World Cup matches ensures the training distribution matches the prediction target.
